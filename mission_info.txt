Artemis II: Human Return to Lunar Orbit
The Artemis II mission is NASA's first crewed flight under the Artemis program, scheduled for no earlier than early 2026. It will carry four astronauts—Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen—on a 10-day journey around the Moon. This mission uses the Space Launch System (SLS) rocket and the Orion spacecraft. Unlike later missions, Artemis II will not land on the lunar surface; instead, it will perform a "hybrid free-return trajectory," looping around the Moon and using gravity to pull the capsule back to Earth for a splashdown in the Pacific Ocean.

James Webb Space Telescope (JWST): 2026 Science Milestones
As of April 2026, the James Webb Space Telescope continues to operate from the second Lagrange point (L2). Recent milestones include the "Cranium Nebula" (PMR 1) imaging in February 2026 and a comprehensive joint study of Saturn with the Hubble Space Telescope in March 2026. A major discovery announced in April 2026 redefined the dividing line between planets and stars by analyzing 29 Cygni b, an object 15 times the mass of Jupiter. The telescope uses its NIRSpec and MIRI instruments to peer through cosmic dust, observing the universe in infrared.

Mars Perseverance Rover: Jezero Crater Exploration
Perseverance landed in Mars' Jezero Crater on February 18, 2021. As of April 20, 2026, it has been active for 1,837 Martian sols. The rover is currently in its "Northern Rim" campaign, exploring the southwestern section of the crater rim to study ancient rocks thrown upward during the original impact. Its primary mission is astrobiology, specifically searching for signs of ancient microbial life and caching rock samples for the future Mars Sample Return (MSR) mission. It is powered by a Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator (MMRTG).

Voyager 1 and 2: The Interstellar Messengers
As of 2026, Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 are the most distant human-made objects, operating more than 13 billion miles from Earth in interstellar space. Both spacecraft are in "survival mode" as their plutonium-based power sources (RTGs) decay by about 4 watts per year. Mission managers have prioritized "fields and particles" instruments to study the heliopause. Despite their age, both remained operational as of April 2026, though engineers anticipate they may become silent by the end of the decade when power levels drop below the threshold required for radio transmission.

Space Communications: The Deep Space Network (DSN)
NASA relies on the Deep Space Network to communicate with missions beyond Earth's orbit. The DSN consists of three main ground stations located in Goldstone (California), Madrid (Spain), and Canberra (Australia). These sites are spaced approximately 120 degrees apart around the globe to ensure that as the Earth rotates, at least one station can maintain a "line of sight" with a spacecraft. Artemis II will also test the Orion Optical Communications System, a laser-based system capable of sending 100 times more data than traditional radio.
